## Writing Rules

All copy on the site follows Poornajith's voice:
- Lead with a question or a specific number
- Short, direct sentences
- No em dashes. Use periods, commas, or restructure.
- No AI vocabulary: crucial, pivotal, vital, showcase, foster, landscape, tapestry, delve, underscore, highlight, enhance, enduring, intricate, meticulous, vibrant, garner, bolster, testament, align with, resonate
- No "not just X, but Y" parallelisms
- No rule-of-three adjective clusters
- No "-ing" phrase analysis tacked onto sentence ends
- No puffery about significance or legacy
- "My take:" and direct questions are fine
- Conversational, specific, grounded in real numbers

### Page 3: My Approach (/approach)

Headline: "How a breakdown gets made"
Subhead: "There's no formula. But there's a rhythm."

Pipeline (visual flow):
Curiosity > Research > Synthesize > Visualize > Publish > Reflect

Phases (expandable):
1. Curiosity: It starts with a question I can't let go of. Usually from work, sometimes from a headline, sometimes from a conversation.
2. Research: YouTube deep dives, industry reports, annual filings. I go until the story connects.
3. Synthesize: Strip it down to what matters. If I can't explain it simply, I don't understand it yet.
4. Visualize: The infographic. I use AI tools (Gemini) with custom prompts to match the Shetty's Desk look.
5. Publish: LinkedIn first. The caption matters as much as the visual. That's where the real thinking lives.
6. Reflect: Who read it? What seniority? What industry? The analytics tell me if the message landed.

Note on the setup: "I'm building a workflow engine to speed up the repetitive parts (research aggregation, prompt templates, analytics tracking) so I can spend more time on the part that actually matters: the thinking."

### Page 4: About Me (/about)

Headline: "The person behind the desk"

Photo

Personal letter:
"Originally from Bangalore. Moved to Sweden for a master's in Logistics & Supply Chain at Lund University, and stayed. I've spent 7+ years at Tetra Pak, starting as a thesis student and now managing replenishment for capital equipment supply chains worth 180M+ euros annually.

Supply chain decisions go wrong not because the data wasn't there. They go wrong because of how decisions get made, who makes them, what they're measuring, and what never makes it into the room in the first place. That gap is why I started Shetty's Desk.

Outside of operations: DE&I Europe Panelist, Culture Champion at Tetra Pak. I believe the same thing about inclusion that I believe about supply chains. The information exists. The gap is always in the translation and execution.

I'm also very interested in people leadership and public speaking. I think the ability to make complex things simple, whether in a planning meeting or on a stage, is the same skill.

'I may not be a scientist, but some of the previous years experiments have yielded positive results.' (Trial and error, they call it.)"

LinkedIn CTA

"What I believe" (expandable cards):
- "The best systems don't need software": The Dabbawalas didn't build a better tracking system. They built a system that doesn't need one. Supply chain complexity is expensive.
- "Leadership is a supply chain problem": Getting the right information to the right person at the right time. That's replenishment. That's also leadership.
- "If both levels engage, the translation worked": When senior directors and entry-level analysts both react to the same post, I know the message landed.
- "Curiosity over perfection": Got inspired by Tiny Experiments by Anne-Laure Le Cunff. I'd rather ship something imperfect and learn than wait for perfect and learn nothing.
- "Inclusion is operational": DE&I isn't a side project. It's the same gap as supply chain: the information exists, the execution doesn't.
